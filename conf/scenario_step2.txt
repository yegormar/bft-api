You are selecting the best scenario and assigning dimension scores for a behavioral assessment.

You receive 3 scenario-based interview questions (title, description, type, options). All 3 were designed to measure the same primary dimension. Your tasks:

1. Choose which of the 3 scenarios is best for this interview. Prefer the scenario that:
   - Best disguises what is being measured (no telegraphing): avoid choosing one whose title, description, or options name the dimension or obvious synonyms (e.g. balance, flexibility, teamwork, responsibility, stretch goal, comfort zone, delegate, free time). The situation and choices should imply the trait without naming it.
   - Has the clearest trade-offs and one clear dilemma, is appropriate for the audience, and feels like a real situation.
2. For the CHOSEN scenario only, assign optionScores: for each option, set dimensionScores (1-5) for the primary dimension. The primary dimension is required.
3. If the chosen scenario and the options allow inferring other traits or values (e.g. a choice also reveals something about collaboration or risk), you may add those dimension IDs and scores to the same optionScores. Include only dimensions that this scenario actually differentiates.

Validation: Scores must be consistent with what each option implies. If an option clearly implies high alignment with a dimension but is scored low, adjust the score. Each score must be an integer from 1 to 5.

When selecting the best scenario, prefer the candidate whose misdirection
is strongest — meaning a test-aware respondent would be least able to name
the dimension being measured. Quality of disguise outweighs structural
tidiness. A messy scenario that hides well beats a clean scenario that
telegraphs the trait.

Prefer the scenario where the surface topic is most different from the
dimension being measured. Reject any candidate that follows the pattern:
"Option A is [X], Option B is [not X] — which do you prefer?"
